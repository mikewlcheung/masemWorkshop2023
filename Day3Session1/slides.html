Example 1: A Five-Factor model (further analysis 1)

  • We use the built-in dataset, so we do not need to reread it.
  • Digman (1997) argued that these two latent factors were independent. Do the data support it?
  • We may compare the model with the constraint (correlation=0) vs. the model without the constraint (correlation≠0) by a chi-square difference test.
    • \(H_0: \rho=0\) vs. \(H_1: \rho \neq 0\)
    • We reject the null hypothesis and prefer the model without the constraint if the p-value is equal to or smaller than .05.
    • We do not reject the null hypothesis and prefer the model with the constraint if the p-value is larger than .05.
  • Result shows that \(\Delta\chi^2(1)=72.86, p<.0001\). Therefore, the latent factors are not independent.

Further analysis 2: R² of the dependent variables

  • Researchers want to report the explained variance (R²), which indicates the percentage of variances explained by the predictors.
  • The definition is \(R^2=\frac{SS_\mathrm{explained}}{SS_\mathrm{total}} = 1- \frac{SS_\mathrm{unexplained}}{SS_\mathrm{total}}\), where \(SS\) is the sum of squares. It equals \(R^2= 1 - \mathrm{Var}(e)\) in correlation analysis.
  • However, we do not have the error variances in our outputs because the error variances are not parameters in the correlation analysis.
  • We may get the error variances by using the diag.constraints=TRUE argument, which forces the program to include the error variances in the models. Nonlinear constraints are applied to ensure that the diagonals are 1.
  • We may use the parameter labels to define the R2 using the := syntax.
  • The estimated R²s are .37 and .39 for W2 and S2, respectively.

Further analysis 3: Likelihood-based vs. Wald confidence intervals

  • Most statistical packages report the Wald confidence intervals (CIs) using the z values.
  • The 95% Wald CI can be easily constructed by using \(estimate \pm 1.96SE\):
    • Advantage: Easy and fast to construct (default in almost all statistical packages)
    • Disadvantage: This may be out of the meaningful bounds, e.g., r > 1 or negative variance.
  • An alternative is the likelihood-based CI (LBCI), which is constructed using the likelihood test:3
    • Advantage: Capture the bounds of the parameters, i.e., r cannot be larger than 1.
    • Disadvantage: time-consuming to create the LBCI.
  • Wald CI is usually sufficient. However, LBCI is preferred when there are functions of parameters.
  • For example, the 95% LBCIs on \(R^2\)s of W2 and S2 are (.33, .42) and (.35, .43), respectively. These CIs provide useful information on the precision of the estimates.

  • MASEM makes it easy to test if the path coefficients are different.
  • We create a dummy variable Pub with Pub=0 for unpublished studies and Pub=1 for published studies.

Correction for unreliability

  • We may also analyze correlations corrected for unreliability if there are theoretical reasons to do it.
  • Different types of reliability measures, e.g., test-retest reliability, coefficient alpha, and coefficient omega, may not be comparable. It is crucial to determine the most appropriate one in the context.
  • Missing data is not allowed in the unreliability. A common approach is to replace the missing value with the mean of the reliabilities.
  • As expected, the estimated path coefficients are usually larger when the corrected correlations are used.
